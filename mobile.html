MOBILE TECHNOLOGY

 

Mobile phones are the most ubiquitous communications technology in the world. Besides transforming the way in which we communicate, they can also be used as a powerful tool for conflict prevention and management.

Mobile services have a proven track record in markets like Kenya, Pakistan and beyond of contributing to economic and social development.

  1. Clearly, mobile services will only be one of several tools when working to overcome political, economic and security problems facing post-conflicts countries. Below we outline some of the key post-conflicts challenges and subsequently explore how mobile services may cont ribute towards overcoming these .

  2. Political challenges and mobile services

    Politically, a post - conflict setting is one where there is a need to reconcile warring parties and address deeper grievances held by the different warring sides. One way to facilitate this is to design n ew inclusive political arrangements where the voice s of all social groups are heard. This might entail amending the constitution and holding free and fair elections. A t a deeper level there will be a need to re-organise political activity away from miltia groups and towards broader political grouping that are more civilian in nature and involve broad segments of the population.Further, goverment institutions are often weak or non-existing and efforts are made to bolster these. These inclucde reforming army, police and judicial structures. It also entails (re-)activating ministries and regional branches of the government administration so as to ensure a government presence not only in large cities but also in rural areas.

  3. The state of Kerala in India is pioneering mobile governance and Kerala’s endeavours may serve as an inspiration to post-conflict countries. Despite high poverty rates, around 80 percent of the population has access to mobile phones and the state is piloting a mechanism for citizens to access government information. Citizens can address the government through voice SMS and data applications. All intra-departmental and inter-departmental notifications happen through an SMS gateway (eSMS). There are also attempts to establish a mobile crime and accident-reporting platform for the police force so as to bolster police efficiency.

  4. Similarly, elections are one step in the transition from war to peace. The electoral process has in many cases proven to be difficult with widespread accusations of fraud and rigging. This undermines the democratisation process and the legitimacy of the new political leadership. Election observation is increasingly making use of mobile platforms that invite citizen monitoring of voting stations in efforts to make elections free and fair. This may be of relevance in post-conflict settings too. By way of example, the Citizens Election Watch-IT (CEWIT) in Uganda received over 40 000 SMS from concerned citizens and these were digested and visualised onto digital maps. This made it possible to expose, in real time, polling stations where irregularities where happening.

  • Economic challenges and mobile services

    1. In economic terms, post-war recovery settings tend to have high rates of poverty and unemployment and the informal and illicit parts of the economy often dominate. Post-war economies also struggle with high outward migration of skilled labour and low rates of foreign direct investment. The government’s tax base is weak due to the large informal economy (which by definition fails to generate government income) and low GDP. A reduced tax base makes it difficult to build a well functioning state. Poverty and unemployment also make continued engagement in militia movements more tempting as these networks may offer salaries or at the very least provide for the upkeep of the soldier.

    2. There are immediate benefits associated with mobile services that support economic development in a range of countries, including those recovering from conflict. Goldman Sachs, among many, emphasise the importance of mobile penetration for growth. They highlight the World Bank finding that for every 10 percent increase in mobile phone penetration, per capita GDP grows by 0.7 percent. The Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) associated with new mobile operations also contributes to recovery. Mobile operators trigger investments, for example through procuring and installing mobile masts and expanding business operations. This generates state revenue and provides private sector employment in the formal economy.

    3. Auctions of mobile licences may also be an important revenue for the state. This kind of revenue is particularly useful for states in the immediate post-war years. In this period, there is often an influx of external financial and material assistance from donors, but these funds are often controlled more by the donors and less by the countries in question. Income from auctions can be “free cash flow” at the disposal of the government. Such processes can also, however, be marred by a lack of transparency, weak governance and corruption.

      4. Perhaps the most important economic contribution associated with mobile phones is the growth of mobile financial services, often labelled mobile banking. This is the provision of ‘branchless banking’ through mobile handsets where users can make fast, easy and cost effective payments and transfers. Mobile banking creates essential financial infrastructure for small-scale entrepreneurs and increases access to capital. It also brings more people into the formal economy and can increase saving within the banking system. These are benefits that are particularly important in post-war settings, where the informal economy tends to be especially large and hinders tax uptake. Moreover, the security situation makes it risky to transfer cash across the country. Mobile transfers offer one solution to this problem.

      Kenya is one of the markets where the mobile banking solutions were pioneered and is today one of the important hubs for innovation in mobile services. 15 An important factor in explaining the rapid uptake and popularity of the mobile banking solutions is the need by workers in the cities to channel remittances to rural home communities. The M-PESA service offers an easy, safe and cheap solution for sending these remittances. Post-war societies often have high rates of displaced people, migrants and uprooted communities. Transfer of funds through mobile banking may therefore be particularly helpful for these groups.

      Finally, aside from facilitating economic transactions, mobile services may also improve the availability of market information to small-scale entrepreneurs and farmers. This addresses an important feature of the post-conflict economy; power imbalances among economic agents. Typically, entrepreneurs that are aligned with, or part of, old militia groups and political factions control some of the key posts in the economy. Large retailers or importers/exporters often have strong political connections and play the role of middlemen who influence price and availability of goods and capital. Individual entrepreneurs and farmers are vulnerable in this situation because they are smaller and unconnected. This skewed power balance may result in unfavourable market terms for small-scale entrepreneurs. However, mobile updates on market demand and local, regional and international prices may go some way in unlocking these relationships. In India, poor farmers have benefited from the Reuters service Reuters Market Light, which offers crop and weather forecasts in combination with price information.In Afghanistan, the mobile operator Roshan is experimenting with a service termed Malomat, which gives Afghan farmers access to price information for over 30 commodities.


    4. Security challenges and mobile services

      5. We see that in many post-conflict settings, even where there has been a formal end to the war and a formal peace settlement, militia groups continue to have access to arms. Moreover, as political violence ends, violence associated with crime rises. Many citizens feel as insecure as they did in the war years. This has two sides. Citizens may feel insecure due to an absence of police and army presence in their areas. Or the reverse, citizens may feel insecure due to this presence. This is because former (violent) strongmen during the war years often gain new formal positions in the police, custom agencies and border guards, while there are few control mechanisms for recovering institutions and levels of impunity may be high. This makes individuals vulnerable to abuse and easy targets for public officers seeking bribes.


      In this situation, a mobile handset is in itself a security boost, as it allows the holder to call for help if threatened or attacked. There is also a growing number of examples where citizens film incidents of abuse by government personnel and post them on the internet.

      More elaborate way to use mobile phones to increase security is found in Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC). The United Nations Development Program (UNDP) reports that as part of a pilot initiative three community representatives (a traditional leader, a woman and an elected official) were given a mobile phone with monthly credits. These representatives report security incidents and human rights abuses through a ‘code sheet’. The information is then aggregated and distributed to government and organisations. This inexpensive solution offers bottom-up generated, yet high quality, data on security threats across the country, which can help the government and humanitarian organisations address safety challenges for the population.


      There are, however, also more specific challenges that tie in with the distinct predicament of a post-conflict situation. These challenges are related to the security situation, risk aversion among customers, the prevalence of fraud and identification problems, misuse of mobile services by warring parties and power imbalances associated with the post-war political set- up. Below we highlight some of the most important challenges in more detail.


      1. Security

        In a post-conflict setting, law and order is weak in many areas. This makes SIM card vendors and mobile banking agents vulnerable to attack. Further, representatives of the mobile operators will have restrictions on movements due to security threats, leading to maintenance delays. To exacerbate this, examples show that mobile masts can be destroyed by insurgents. This leads to high operational costs for mobile operators in post-conflict settings.

      2. Risk-averse customers

        The experiences of war may discourage long-term planning, and in turn, the effort and interest in trying new services. Citizens may have experienced commercial services that have come and gone under different regimes, while a few resilient services, perhaps traditional ones, have endured. So while the potential benefits and suitability of mobile solutions in post-conflict setting may be obvious on paper, a risk averse population may want to avoid the start-up costs and the perceived risks of new commercial offerings.

      3. IDs and fraud

        Obtaining formal identity cards and passports is often a bottleneck in situations where the government is not running efficiently. This makes it difficult to identify and register new customers. In turn, counterfeits in registrations and attempts to fraud services such as mobile banking flourish.

      4. Mobile services form part of the conflict

        Mobile services providers often find that new solutions are proving as useful for criminal groups and spoilers of the peace as they are to the segments of society that are trying to move forward in the post-war period. By way of example, mobile banking services have reportedly assisted money transfers of Somali pirates, and the Islamic militant group in Somalia, Al-Shabaab, are active Twitter users. Another example is how call or SMS records on individual phones can be used to track movements or prove allegiance to a particular group or party which in turn can be used to control or in threats or extortion.

      5. Power, regulation and hostility to scaling up

        Above, we noted that a solid regulatory framework facilitates the launch of mobile services. However, as a society transitions from war to peace, new regulation may be difficult to introduce. Politicians and top government may not have the time or capacity to attend to such regulatory issues among other key tasks such as forging a new constitution or deciding on who should get to form government. Another difficult challenge stems from how the war years have often concentrated power in the hands of a select group of individuals or institutions. These may often wield their power unchecked. New technological services that can help mitigate this may be met with resistance. One example is how the provision of market information to small-scale entrepreneurs helps challenge monopolies, but as a consequence also key vested interests. This may lead to pressure from powerful economic or political interests to stall or delay such services. This makes the launch and uptake of new services in a post-conflict setting particularly difficult.
